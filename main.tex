\begin{abstract}
This study examines how behavioral interaction features influence user satisfaction and continued usage in AI-assisted learning systems. Using a synthetic dataset modeling student study sessions, a set of normalized behavioral metrics—Engagement, Persistence, Efficiency, and Friction Tolerance—was constructed to capture interaction dynamics beyond raw performance outcomes. 

A Random Forest classifier was trained to predict continued usage, achieving an accuracy of 72.5\% and an ROC AUC score of 0.65. Feature importance analysis revealed that Efficiency is the dominant predictor of retention, significantly outweighing raw effort-based metrics such as Persistence and Friction Tolerance. 

The findings suggest that users prioritize achieving high-quality outcomes with minimal interaction cost, rather than maximizing effort or engagement alone. These results highlight the importance of designing AI-driven learning systems that optimize for efficiency and perceived effectiveness, supporting both successful outcomes and sustained user adoption.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Artificial intelligence is increasingly integrated into educational environments, fundamentally reshaping how students interact with learning resources. Modern AI-driven systems provide on-demand assistance, adaptive feedback, and personalized guidance, but their effectiveness depends not only on the availability of these tools, but on how learners engage with them. Understanding behavioral interaction patterns is therefore essential for evaluating both learning outcomes and long-term system adoption.

Prior research has focused on performance metrics or user satisfaction, less attention being given to the behavioral features that appear during interaction, such as engagement intensity, persistence, and efficiency. The behavioral signals can offer deeper insight into how students balance effort, reliance on AI assistance, and perceived outcomes during study sessions.

~This study investigates the role of behavioral metrics in predicting user satisfaction and continued usage of AI-assisted learning systems. Using a structured dataset of simulated study sessions,  normalized indicators are constructed in such a way that they capture engagement, persistence, efficiency, and friction tolerance, which are later analyzed so their impact on retention and satisfaction can be approximated.

The results highlight efficiency as the dominant predictor of continued usage, suggesting that users prioritize outcome quality relative to effort over raw interaction volume. These findings contribute to the design of more effective and user-centered AI learning systems.




\section{Dataset and Research Design}
\subsection{Preprocessing and Definitions}

To ensure comparability across variables with different scales, raw interaction features are standardized using Z-score normalization:

\[
Z(x) = \frac{x - \mu}{\sigma}
\]

where \( \mu \) and \( \sigma \) denote the mean and standard deviation of the feature, respectively.

Outcome-related variables are transformed using Min-Max scaling to map values to the interval [0,1]:

\[
\text{MinMax}(x) = \frac{x - x_{\min}}{x_{\max} - x_{\min}}
\]

This ensures strictly positive values, which are required for ratio-based feature construction.

For stability in division operations, a small constant \( \epsilon = 0.01 \) is introduced to prevent division by zero. Additionally, the Engagement metric is shifted to ensure strictly positive values:

\[
\text{Engagement}_{\text{shifted}} = \text{Engagement} + |\min(\text{Engagement})| + \epsilon
\]

These transformations ensure numerical stability and prevent scale-related distortions in the derived behavioral metrics.

The dataset represents a synthetic dataset that models student interaction with AI-assisted learning systems. The dataset contains 11 fields with data about collected during AI-assisted study sessions.The dataset used in this study was obtained from Kaggle \cite{ai_learning_dataset}. Before any analysis was done, the data was cleaned(unnecessary fields were removed, non-numerical fields were replaced with numerical representations) and extra features were computed based on the given features (Engagement, Persistence, Efficiency and Friction tolerance), each feature constructed to positively or negatively weight behavioral signals.

\section{Building Behavioral Metrics}

~The behavioral dimensions considered in this study are conceptually grounded in prior work on the ThinkR platform, where interaction-based features were shown to reflect cognitive and motivational aspects of learning behavior \cite{penrose_article}.

\subsection{Engagement (Interaction Intensity)}

Engagement captures the total volume of interaction, balancing both time spent and prompt frequency, . The engagement feature is rewarding each prompt and each minute spent studying. The formula of engagement is the following:

    \[
    \text{Engagement} = Z(\text{TotalPrompts}) + Z(\text{SessionLengthMin})
    \]

\subsection{Persistence (Cognitive Effort)}

Persistence measures sustained effort, it gently penalizes over-reliance on AI assistance (which requires less independent cognitive effort). The persistence feature rewards the effort, the work, but also makes sure the student put in cognitive effort.The coefficient (0.3) was empirically chosen to moderate the influence of AI assistance, ensuring that assistance moderates but does not dominate the persistence signal. This reflects the assumption that moderate AI use supports learning, while excessive reliance reduces independent cognitive effort.The formula of persistence is the following:

    \[
    \text{Persistence} = Z(\text{TotalPrompts}) - (0.3 \times Z(\text{AI\_AssistanceLevel}))
    \]

\subsection{Efficiency (Outcome Relative to Effort)}

Efficiency computes the successful output realative to engagement, it is modeled as outcome quality relative to interaction cost. Both features that contribute to efficiency (the final outcome and the satisfaction rating) are optimised and normalised so they ensure objective performance and subjective perception.The formula of efficiency is the following:

    \[
    \text{Efficiency} = \frac{MinMax(\text{FinalOutcome}) \times MinMax(\text{SatisfactionRating})}{\text{Engagement}_{\text{shifted}} + \epsilon}
    \]

\subsection{Friction Tolerance (pure effort)}

The Friction Tolerance metric captures cognitive effort, by penalizing reliance on AI assistance. Higher values indicate users who engage actively while maintaining lower dependence on automated support. While less expressive than composite features, it serves as a baseline indicator of autonomy in interaction behavior.The formula of friction tolerance is the following:

    \[
     \text{FrictionTolerance} = \text{TotalPrompts} - \text{AI\_AssistanceLevel}
    \]

    

Together, these metrics form a structured behavioral representation of user interaction, balancing effort, outcome, and assistance. This feature space enables the subsequent analysis of their relative importance in predicting satisfaction and continued usage.

\section{Results and Discussion}

Following the extraction and normalization of behavioral features, their relationships with learning outcomes, user satisfaction, and continued usage were analyzed. Both exploratory analysis and predictive modeling were employed to identify the most influential behavioral signals.

\subsection{Behavioral Trends}

\textbf{Engagement.}
Engagement exhibits a strong positive relationship with both learning outcomes and user satisfaction. Higher engagement scores, derived from increased interaction frequency and session duration, consistently correspond to improved final outcomes. This trend suggests that active and sustained interaction with AI-assisted systems contributes positively to task completion and perceived effectiveness. The presence of outliers, captured through Z-score normalization, reflects meaningful behavioral variation rather than noise, indicating distinct user interaction patterns.

\textbf{Persistence.}
Persistence demonstrates a clear positive association with successful task completion. Users who achieve higher final outcomes tend to exhibit higher persistence scores, reflecting sustained effort and continued interaction. However, an inverse relationship is observed between persistence and satisfaction. Users reporting the highest satisfaction levels tend to require less persistence, suggesting that prolonged effort may be associated with friction or difficulty during interaction. This highlights a trade-off between effort and user experience: persistence supports success, but excessive persistence may reduce perceived usability.

\textbf{Efficiency.}
Efficiency presents the most distinctive behavioral pattern. While lower and intermediate outcome categories show relatively low and stable efficiency values, a sharp increase is observed for sessions resulting in successful task completion. Similarly, higher satisfaction levels strongly correlate with increased efficiency. This indicates that users derive the greatest satisfaction when achieving high-quality outcomes with minimal interaction cost. Efficiency therefore captures the balance between effort and outcome, making it a critical indicator of effective system use.

\subsection{Predictive Modeling of Continued Usage}

To quantify the impact of behavioral features on user retention, a Random Forest classifier was trained to predict continued usage (binary variable \textit{UsedAgain}). The dataset was split into training and testing subsets using an 80/20 ratio, with stratification to preserve class distribution. Hyperparameter optimization was performed using randomized search with cross-validation.

The optimized model achieved an accuracy of 72.5\% and an ROC AUC score of 0.65, indicating moderate predictive capability. While overall performance is satisfactory, class-level analysis reveals an imbalance in predictive sensitivity. The model achieved a recall of 0.25 for non-returning users, indicating limited detection of disengagement cases. However, precision for this class reached 0.57, suggesting that flagged at-risk users are identified with relatively high reliability.

\subsection{Feature Importance Analysis}

Feature importance analysis derived from the trained Random Forest model highlights the relative contribution of each behavioral metric:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Efficiency} is the dominant predictor, contributing approximately 40\% of the model's decision weight.
    \item \textbf{Engagement} (22\%) and \textbf{Satisfaction Rating} (18\%) serve as strong secondary predictors.
    \item \textbf{Persistence} and \textbf{Friction Tolerance} contribute the least (approximately 10\% each).
\end{itemize}

These results indicate that continued usage is primarily driven by the efficiency of interaction rather than raw effort. Users are significantly more likely to return when their interactions yield high-quality outcomes relative to the time and effort invested.

\subsection{Interpretation}

The findings suggest that behavioral efficiency acts as a unifying signal that captures both performance and user experience. While engagement and persistence remain necessary components of successful interaction, they do not independently guarantee continued usage. Instead, users prioritize systems that enable them to achieve their objectives with minimal friction.

This emphasizes the importance of designing AI-assisted learning systems that optimize not only for engagement, but for outcome efficiency and perceived effectiveness. Systems that reduce unnecessary interaction overhead while maintaining high-quality outputs are more likely to sustain long-term user adoption.
